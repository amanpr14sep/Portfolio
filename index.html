01 — About

The strategist's job is to notice what the brand can't see about itself.

I think in audiences, not demographics — in the fifteen minutes someone actually gives a brand, not the hour it wants.

Strategy excites me because it sits at the exact point where a brand's ambition meets a person's attention span — and someone has to decide what happens there. That's the job I want: reading a category, finding the insight no one's written down yet, and turning it into a plan a client can actually run.

My training has been split between numbers and narrative. Days spent building BSL and SOV trackers taught me that a media plan is only as good as the pattern it's built on. Days spent in brand strategy sessions for Kia and Realme taught me that the pattern means nothing if it can't be told as a story in one slide.

How I approach a brief
01

Read the category first

Before touching a media plan, I map who else is talking, where, and how loudly — competitive share of voice sets the baseline for everything after.

02

Find the human insight

Consumer research isn't a checkbox — it's where the actual campaign idea usually hides, if you're reading the transcripts closely enough.

03

Build the plan around behaviour

TV, print, digital — the mix follows how people actually move through their day, not how the media owner sells inventory.

04

Make it presentable

A strategy that can't survive a client room in twelve slides isn't finished yet. Clarity is the last mile of good thinking.
